Featured Research

When the Smoke Clears: How Tobacco Taxation Causally Impacts Child Health

Revise & Resubmit

Does tobacco taxation improve child health? This paper exploits China's 2015 tobacco tax reform — which raised the ad valorem excise tax from 5% to 11% and introduced a new specific excise tax — as a quasi-natural experiment. Using a difference-in-differences design with data from the China Family Panel Studies (2010–2022), I compare health outcomes of children in smoking households versus non-smoking households before and after the reform.

Key Findings

  • Children in smoking households experienced a 0.14 standard-deviation increase in Height-for-Age Z-scores after the reform, narrowing the gap to the WHO reference by approximately 8%.
  • Annual medical visits for children in treated households declined by 15.7%, suggesting improved overall health status.
  • The health gains are largest for low-income families, multi-child households, and preschool-aged children, indicating the policy is health-progressive.

Mechanisms

The reform operates through a "double dividend": (1) reduced secondhand smoke exposure improves parental health and labor productivity, increasing household income; (2) savings from lower tobacco expenditure are reallocated toward child welfare — food expenditure shares and children's pocket money both increase significantly. No evidence of crowding-out is found.
